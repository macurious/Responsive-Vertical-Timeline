• 06/1955

    Tim Berners-Lee is born in London, England.

  • 1976

    He graduates from The Queen's College, Oxford, with a degree in physics.

  • 1980

    While working as a contractor at CERN, he builds ENQUIRE, an early hypertext prototype.

  • 1984

    He returns to CERN as a fellow, where large-scale information sharing becomes a key challenge.

  • 03/1989

    He submits his proposal at CERN for a distributed hypertext system that becomes the World Wide Web.

  • 11/1990

    With Robert Cailliau, he formalizes the web project and begins implementing core technologies.

  • 12/1990

    He creates the first web browser/editor and the first web server at CERN.

  • 08/1991

    He announces the World Wide Web project publicly on the alt.hypertext newsgroup.

  • 12/1991

    The first web server outside Europe is deployed at SLAC in the United States.

  • 04/1993

    CERN places web technology in the public domain, accelerating global adoption.

  • 10/1994

    He founds the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) at MIT to guide open web standards.

  • 2004

    He is knighted by Queen Elizabeth II for services to the global development of the internet.

  • 2009

    He helps launch the World Wide Web Foundation to promote an open web as a public good.

  • 2012

    He supports open data through the UK Government's Open Data Institute, founded with Nigel Shadbolt.

  • 2014

    He receives the inaugural Queen Elizabeth Prize for Engineering for his role in creating the web.

  • 2016

    He publicly pushes for stronger privacy and net neutrality protections worldwide.

  • 2018

    He launches Inrupt to support Solid, his project for user-controlled personal data on the web.

  • 2019

    He introduces the Contract for the Web, urging governments, companies, and citizens to protect the open web.

  • 2021

    He continues advocacy for decentralized, privacy-respecting web architecture through Solid and W3C work.

  • 2023

    He marks over three decades of the web while emphasizing openness, interoperability, and user rights.

  • Today

    As W3C Director Emeritus, he remains a leading voice for a web that is open, inclusive, and trustworthy.
